# LoanXAI — Loan & Credit Risk Glossary

Complete reference for every financial and technical term used in this project.
Written in plain English for Indian borrowers and final year project readers.

---

## Financial Terms

### CIBIL Score
| | |
|---|---|
| **Full form** | Credit Information Bureau (India) Limited Score |
| **Range** | 300 – 900 |
| **Model feature** | EXT_SOURCE_1, EXT_SOURCE_2, EXT_SOURCE_3 (normalised 0–1) |
| **Ideal value** | 750 and above |

**What it means:**
A 3-digit number summarising your entire borrowing history. Generated by TransUnion CIBIL — India's oldest credit bureau (est. 2000). Also available from Experian, Equifax, and CRIF Highmark.

**How it is calculated:**
- Payment history (35%) — did you pay EMIs and credit card bills on time?
- Credit utilisation (30%) — are you using too much of your credit limit?
- Length of credit history (15%) — older accounts are better
- Credit mix (10%) — having both secured and unsecured loans helps
- New enquiries (10%) — too many loan applications hurt the score

**Example:**
Anita has a CIBIL of 790. Every bank offers her the lowest interest rate and fastest approval because she has paid every EMI on time for 8 years.

---

### EMI (Equated Monthly Instalment)
| | |
|---|---|
| **Formula** | EMI = P × r × (1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n − 1) |
| **Model feature** | AMT_ANNUITY |

**What it means:**
The fixed amount you pay every month to repay a loan. It stays the same throughout the loan period (for fixed-rate loans). Each EMI contains two parts:
1. **Principal repayment** — reduces the loan balance
2. **Interest payment** — the bank's profit for lending money

In the early months, most of the EMI is interest. As time passes, more of each EMI goes to reducing the principal.

**Example:**
₹5,00,000 loan at 12% p.a. for 5 years → EMI = ₹11,122/month for 60 months.
Total paid = ₹6,67,320. Interest paid = ₹1,67,320.

---

### Principal
| | |
|---|---|
| **Model feature** | AMT_CREDIT |

**What it means:**
The original amount of money borrowed before any interest is added. As you pay EMIs each month, the outstanding principal decreases. Your bank statement shows the "outstanding principal" or "loan balance" remaining.

**Example:**
You borrow ₹3L to buy a two-wheeler. ₹3L is the principal. Total repayment with interest = ~₹3.9L.

---

### Interest Rate (Rate of Interest / ROI)
**What it means:**
The annual cost of borrowing money as a percentage. A 12% p.a. rate means you pay 12% of the outstanding loan as interest per year.

**Types:**
- **Fixed rate** — same throughout loan period. Predictable EMIs.
- **Floating rate** — changes with RBI repo rate. Can go up or down.

**Typical Indian rates (2024–25):**

| Loan Type | Rate Range |
|---|---|
| Personal loan | 10.5% – 24% p.a. |
| Home loan | 8.4% – 12% p.a. |
| Car loan | 7.5% – 14% p.a. |
| Gold loan | 7% – 15% p.a. |
| Education loan | 8% – 15% p.a. |
| PM Mudra loan | 8% – 12% p.a. |

---

### Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)
| | |
|---|---|
| **Formula** | DTI = (Total Monthly EMIs ÷ Monthly Income) × 100 |
| **Ideal value** | Below 40% |
| **Model feature** | Derived from AMT_ANNUITY vs AMT_INCOME_TOTAL |

**What it means:**
The percentage of your monthly income that goes toward all loan repayments. A high DTI means you are financially stretched and may not be able to handle additional debt.

**Example:**
- Monthly income: ₹50,000
- Car loan EMI: ₹8,000
- Personal loan EMI: ₹5,000
- New loan EMI requested: ₹10,000
- Total EMIs: ₹23,000
- DTI = (23,000 ÷ 50,000) × 100 = **46% → Too high. Most banks will reject.**

---

### Loan-to-Value Ratio (LTV)
| | |
|---|---|
| **Formula** | LTV = (Loan Amount ÷ Asset Value) × 100 |

**RBI Guidelines (2024):**

| Home Loan Amount | Max LTV |
|---|---|
| Up to ₹30 lakh | 90% |
| ₹30L – ₹75L | 80% |
| Above ₹75 lakh | 75% |
| Gold loan | 75% |

**Example:**
House worth ₹60L. Bank lends ₹48L (LTV = 80%). You pay ₹12L as down payment from your own savings.

---

### Collateral / Security
| | |
|---|---|
| **Model feature** | FLAG_OWN_REALTY_Y, FLAG_OWN_CAR_Y |

**What it means:**
An asset pledged as security for a loan. If you cannot repay, the lender legally takes and sells the collateral to recover the money.

**Types:**
- **Immovable:** Land, house, commercial property
- **Movable:** Gold jewellery, vehicle, machinery
- **Financial:** Fixed deposits, insurance policies, shares

**Secured vs Unsecured:**
- **Secured (with collateral):** Home loan, car loan, gold loan, LAP
- **Unsecured (no collateral):** Personal loan, credit card, Mudra loan

---

### Guarantor / Co-applicant
| | |

**Guarantor:** Third person who signs the loan and promises to repay if the borrower defaults. Their CIBIL score is checked separately.

**Co-applicant:** Applies jointly. Equally responsible for repayment. Combined income is counted.

**When required:** Low CIBIL score, insufficient income, no credit history (first-time borrowers).

**Risk:** If the borrower defaults, the guarantor's CIBIL score is damaged equally.

**Example:**
Meena (CIBIL 630) asks her husband (CIBIL 780) to be co-applicant. The joint profile gets the loan approved.

---

### Processing Fee
**What it means:**
A one-time, non-refundable administrative fee charged by the lender for processing your application. Even if you later cancel the loan or get rejected, this fee is not returned.

**Range:** 0.5% – 3% of loan amount

**Example:**
₹10L loan with 1.5% processing fee = ₹15,000. Your bank account receives only ₹9,85,000.

---

### Pre-payment / Foreclosure
**Pre-payment:** Paying extra money to reduce the outstanding principal faster.

**Foreclosure:** Paying the entire remaining loan in one shot before the end of tenure.

**Penalty:** Fixed-rate loans: 2–5% of outstanding amount. RBI mandates zero prepayment penalty on floating-rate loans after 2012.

**Example:**
5-year ₹5L loan. In year 2, after bonus, you foreclose. You save ₹60,000 in future interest that you would have paid in remaining 3 years.

---

### NPA (Non-Performing Asset)
| | |
|---|---|
| **Model feature** | DEF_30_CNT_SOCIAL_CIRCLE, DEF_60_CNT_SOCIAL_CIRCLE |

**What it means:**
When a borrower misses EMI payments for 90 consecutive days, the bank reclassifies the loan as NPA. This is a serious event for both lender and borrower.

**Impact on borrower:**
- CIBIL score drops 100–200 points immediately
- Negative entry stays on CIBIL report for 7 years
- All future loan applications from all banks get rejected
- Bank can initiate legal action under SARFAESI Act
- For secured loans: bank can seize and sell collateral without court order

**Example:**
Anil missed EMI for 4 months. Loan became NPA. CIBIL dropped from 710 to 490. All 8 banks he applied to after that rejected him.

---

### Credit Enquiry (Hard Pull)
| | |
|---|---|
| **Model feature** | AMT_REQ_CREDIT_BUREAU_YEAR, _QRT, _MON, _WEEK |

**What it means:**
When you formally apply for a loan, the lender submits a request to CIBIL to view your credit report. This is recorded as a "hard enquiry" and is visible to all future lenders who check your report.

**Impact:** Each hard enquiry reduces your score by ~2–8 points. Multiple enquiries signal financial desperation.

**Advice:** Do not apply to 6 banks simultaneously. Apply to 1–2 lenders at a time.

**Example:**
Raju applied to 6 banks in 3 months. All 6 enquiries are visible on his CIBIL report. Score dropped 40 points even though no loan was sanctioned.

---

### NBFC (Non-Banking Finance Company)
| | |
|---|---|
| **Examples** | Bajaj Finance, Muthoot, HDFC Ltd, Shriram Finance, Mahindra Finance |
| **Regulated by** | Reserve Bank of India (RBI) |

**Key difference from banks:** Cannot accept current/savings deposits. Can lend money. Cannot issue cheque books.

**When to go to NBFC:** Bank rejected you. CIBIL between 550–700. Need faster approval. Self-employed with irregular income.

**Trade-off:** Higher interest rates (typically +3% to +8% over banks). Faster processing.

---

### Moratorium
**What it means:**
A lender-granted temporary pause on EMI payments during financial hardship. Interest continues to accumulate during this period.

**Practical impact:** After moratorium ends, either your EMI increases or your tenure extends to account for unpaid interest.

**Example:**
COVID-19: RBI announced 6-month moratorium (March–August 2020). Priya paused EMIs but paid ₹28,000 more in total due to accumulated interest.

### Kisan Credit Card (KCC)
**Target:** Farmers for short-term agricultural credit.

**Interest rate:** 4% p.a. for loans up to ₹3 lakh (government provides interest subvention).

**Purpose:** Seeds, fertiliser, pesticides, farm equipment maintenance, harvest expenses.

---

### Stand Up India Scheme
**Target:** SC/ST borrowers and women entrepreneurs only.

**Amount:** ₹10 lakh to ₹1 crore per borrower.

**Purpose:** Only for setting up greenfield (brand new) enterprises in manufacturing, services, or trading.

**Guarantee:** Covered under CGFSIL (Credit Guarantee Fund Scheme for Stand Up India Loans). Collateral-free.

## ML / XAI Technical Terms

### XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting)
**What it is:**
An ensemble machine learning algorithm. Builds hundreds of decision trees sequentially — each new tree tries to fix the errors made by all previous trees. Final prediction is the weighted sum of all trees.

**Why it's best for credit risk:**
- Handles missing values natively (critical in loan datasets)
- Fast training and prediction
- Naturally captures non-linear relationships (e.g., low income is fine if assets are high)
- Excellent AUC-ROC performance on tabular data
- Full SHAP support

**In this project:** Trained on 228 features from Home Credit Default Risk dataset.

---

### SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)
**What it is:**
A technique from cooperative game theory applied to ML model explanations. For any single prediction, SHAP calculates how much each feature "contributed" to pushing the prediction above or below the base rate.

**Mathematical basis:**
```
Prediction = Base Rate + SHAP(feature_1) + SHAP(feature_2) + ... + SHAP(feature_228)
```

**Interpretation in this project:**
- **Negative SHAP value** → this feature lowers default probability → **GOOD for applicant**
- **Positive SHAP value** → this feature raises default probability → **BAD for applicant**
- **Larger absolute value** → stronger effect on this prediction

**Example:**
```
Base rate (avg default): 8%
EXT_SOURCE_2 SHAP:       -0.24  (strong CIBIL → good)
AMT_CREDIT SHAP:         +0.18  (high loan → bad)
DAYS_EMPLOYED SHAP:      -0.12  (stable job → good)
---
Final prediction: ~28.5% default risk → APPROVED
```

**Why SHAP over other methods:**
- **Locally accurate** — SHAP values always sum to exactly the prediction
- **Per-prediction** — every applicant gets their own explanation
- **Regulation-ready** — RBI's Fair Practice Code requires lenders to explain rejections

### AUC-ROC
| | |
|---|---|
| **Range** | 0.5 (random) to 1.0 (perfect) |
| **This model** | ~0.91 |

**What it means:** The model correctly ranks a defaulter above a non-defaulter 91% of the time. In practice, AUC > 0.80 is good for credit scoring, > 0.85 is excellent.

### EXT_SOURCE Features
| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| EXT_SOURCE_1 | Credit score from external bureau 1 (normalised 0–1) |
| EXT_SOURCE_2 | Credit score from external bureau 2 (normalised 0–1) |
| EXT_SOURCE_3 | Credit score from external bureau 3 (normalised 0–1) |

**In Indian context:** These map to scores from CIBIL, Experian India, and CRIF Highmark.

**Why most important:** They are the strongest predictors of default in the model. Higher score = lower default risk.

**How we map CIBIL:** `EXT_SOURCE = (CIBIL_score - 300) / 600` → maps 300→0, 900→1.

---

### Class Imbalance
**Problem:** In the Home Credit dataset, only ~8% of loans defaulted. A naive model could get 92% accuracy by predicting "no default" for everyone — but it would fail at catching the 8% who actually default.

**Solutions used:**
- `scale_pos_weight = 10` in XGBoost (penalises misclassifying minority class more)
- SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) during training
- Evaluate using AUC-ROC and Precision-Recall, not just accuracy
